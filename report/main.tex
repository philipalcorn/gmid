\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a Python-based simulation pipeline that automates the
gm/Id transistor sizing methodology for CMOS analog circuits.  The tool
characterizes NMOS and PMOS transistors across channel length, gate-to-source
voltage, drain-to-source voltage, and bulk-to-source voltage using LTspice
operating-point sweeps, stores the results in a four-dimensional look-up
table (LUT), and provides a query interface for transistor sizing.  The
design flow is demonstrated by applying the extracted LUTs to the sizing of
a folded-cascode class-AB operational amplifier, walking through each
transistor group from specification to final width.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
gm/Id methodology, CMOS amplifier design, LTspice automation, look-up
table, folded-cascode amplifier, class-AB output stage, transistor sizing
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Introductory analog circuit courses use the square-law MOSFET model to
derive sizing equations.  The square-law predicts that in saturation

\begin{equation}
  I_D = \frac{1}{2} \mu_n C_{ox} \frac{W}{L} (V_{GS} - V_{th})^2,
  \label{eq:squarelaw}
\end{equation}

and sizing a transistor means solving \eqref{eq:squarelaw} for $W/L$ given
a target $I_D$ and an assumed overdrive $V_{ov} = V_{GS} - V_{th}$.  In
practice, modern SPICE models include effects that make the real device
behave differently from this simple equation, so a transistor sized by hand
from \eqref{eq:squarelaw} will not match its simulated operating point.
Designers then iterate between hand calculations and simulation until the
numbers converge, which is time-consuming.

The gm/Id methodology~\cite{silveira1996} bypasses this iteration by
replacing the square-law with data taken directly from the SPICE model.
Rather than computing $W$ from an equation, the designer looks up how much
current a given width carries at the desired bias condition and scales
accordingly.  Because the data already include all of the model's
non-ideal behavior, no iteration is needed.  Murmann's lecture
notes~\cite{murmann2020} and Tiwari's design slides~\cite{tiwari} provide
accessible introductions to the methodology; Allen and Holberg~\cite{allen2011}
give a thorough treatment of the circuit topologies used in the design
example.

This work implements the gm/Id workflow in Python on top of
PyLTSpice~\cite{pyltspice}, enabling automated parametric sweeps,
structured data extraction, and an interactive query interface.
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} explains the gm/Id concept.
Section~\ref{sec:tool} describes the software.
Section~\ref{sec:characterization} covers the characterization sweep.
Section~\ref{sec:lut} explains the LUT and how sizing queries work.
Section~\ref{sec:folded_cascode} applies the tool to a complete
folded-cascode class-AB amplifier design.

\section{The gm/Id Design Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Transconductance Efficiency}

The transconductance $g_m$ of a MOSFET is defined as the change in drain
current per change in gate voltage, $g_m = \partial I_D / \partial V_{GS}$.
Dividing by the quiescent drain current gives the \textit{transconductance
efficiency}:

\begin{equation}
  \frac{g_m}{I_D} \;\left[\text{V}^{-1}\right].
  \label{eq:gmid}
\end{equation}

This ratio answers the question: \textit{how much small-signal gain does
this transistor produce per unit of DC current?}  A high $g_m/I_D$ means
the device is power-efficient; a low $g_m/I_D$ means the device is fast
but draws more current for the same $g_m$.

From the square-law, $g_m = 2 I_D / V_{ov}$, so

\begin{equation}
  \frac{g_m}{I_D}\bigg|_{\text{sq.law}} = \frac{2}{V_{ov}}.
  \label{eq:gmid_sq}
\end{equation}

As $V_{GS}$ increases (device driven harder into strong inversion), $V_{ov}$
grows and $g_m/I_D$ falls.  As $V_{GS}$ approaches $V_{th}$ the device
enters weak inversion (subthreshold), where $I_D$ is exponential and
$g_m/I_D$ reaches its maximum of roughly $25$--$35\,\text{V}^{-1}$ for a
typical process.

In reality, $g_m/I_D$ is a smooth, monotonically decreasing function of
$V_{GS}$ that does not follow \eqref{eq:gmid_sq} exactly.  The LUT captures
this real curve directly from simulation data.

\subsection{What the gm/Id Value Tells You}

Choosing a $g_m/I_D$ target is equivalent to choosing where on the
weak-inversion to strong-inversion continuum the transistor will be biased:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $g_m/I_D \approx 25\,\text{V}^{-1}$: weak inversion.  Maximum
        power efficiency.  The transistor needs a small $V_{GS}$ above
        $V_{th}$ and draws very little current, but it is slow and
        $V_{dsat}$ is large (poor output swing).
  \item $g_m/I_D \approx 15\,\text{V}^{-1}$: moderate inversion.  Good
        balance of efficiency and speed.  A practical starting point for
        most signal-path transistors.
  \item $g_m/I_D \approx 5$--$10\,\text{V}^{-1}$: strong inversion.  Low
        efficiency, but the device is fast and $V_{dsat}$ is small, which
        is useful for output-stage transistors where output swing matters.
\end{itemize}

Once a $g_m/I_D$ target is chosen, the LUT gives the drain current per
unit width ($I_D/W$) at that operating point.  Knowing the required
$I_D$ from the circuit specification, the width follows directly:

\begin{equation}
  W = \frac{I_{D,\text{required}}}{(I_D / W)_{\text{LUT}}}.
  \label{eq:width}
\end{equation}

No iteration is needed.

\subsection{Key Design Variables in the LUT}

For each bias point the LUT stores the following quantities (per unit
width, for $W_{\text{ref}} = 10\,\mu\text{m}$):

\begin{itemize}
  \item $I_D$, $g_m$, $g_{ds}$ --- drain current, transconductance, and
        output conductance from the SPICE operating-point solver.
  \item $g_m/I_D$ --- transconductance efficiency (computed).
  \item $I_D/W$ --- current density (computed from $I_D$ and $W_{\text{ref}}$).
  \item $g_m/g_{ds}$ --- intrinsic gain; useful for estimating $A_v$ of a
        single-transistor stage.
  \item $f_T = g_m / (2\pi C_{gg})$ --- transition frequency; a measure of
        how fast the transistor is.
  \item $V_{th}$, $V_{dsat}$ --- threshold and saturation voltage.
\end{itemize}

\section{Software Architecture}
\label{sec:tool}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The pipeline is implemented in Python and uses PyLTSpice~\cite{pyltspice}
to drive LTspice.  Four modules handle the workflow.

\textbf{\texttt{spice.py}} wraps the PyLTSpice simulation runner.  It
converts the \texttt{.asc} schematic to a netlist, updates \texttt{.param}
values for each sweep point, and runs up to 20 LTspice processes in
parallel.

\textbf{\texttt{callbacks.py}} runs after each simulation finishes.  It
reads the \texttt{.log} operating-point data and the \texttt{.raw} waveform
file, and bundles everything into a \texttt{SimResult} object keyed by
device name.

\textbf{\texttt{parser.py}} converts a list of \texttt{SimResult} objects
into structured \texttt{ParsedSimResult} objects containing the traces,
measurements, and device parameters the designer requested.

\textbf{\texttt{characterization.py}} coordinates the full characterization
sweep, assembles results into four-dimensional NumPy arrays indexed by
$(L, V_{GS}, V_{DS}, V_{SB})$, and saves them to \texttt{.npz} files.
\textbf{\texttt{query\_lut.py}} loads a saved LUT and answers sizing
queries via command-line arguments or an interactive terminal prompt.

\section{Device Characterization Sweep}
\label{sec:characterization}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Testbench Topology}

The characterization schematic (\texttt{nmos\_char.asc}) contains one NMOS
(M1) and one PMOS (M2) transistor.  Both are characterized in a single
operating-point simulation by mirroring the NMOS bias about the supply rail
for the PMOS:

\begin{align}
  V_{g,p} &= V_{DD} - V_{gs}, \\
  V_{d,p} &= V_{DD} - V_{ds}, \\
  V_{b,p} &= V_{DD} - V_b.
\end{align}

This means that sweeping $(V_{gs}, V_{ds})$ for the NMOS simultaneously
sweeps the intrinsic $(V_{SG}, V_{SD})$ of the PMOS, so one Python loop
produces LUTs for both device types.

\subsection{Sweep Parameters}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $L \in \{0.35, 0.70, 1.00, 2.00\}\,\mu\text{m}$
  \item $V_{GS}$ from $0$ to $5\,\text{V}$ in $50\,\text{mV}$ steps (101 points)
  \item $V_{DS}$ from $0$ to $5\,\text{V}$ in $50\,\text{mV}$ steps (101 points)
  \item $V_{SB} \in \{0, 0.45, 0.90\}\,\text{V}$
  \item Reference width $W_{\text{ref}} = 10\,\mu\text{m}$
\end{itemize}

This produces $4 \times 101 \times 101 \times 3 = 122\,412$ operating-point
simulations run in parallel across 20 LTspice worker processes.

\section{Look-Up Table and Sizing Queries}
\label{sec:lut}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{LUT Structure}

After all simulations complete, the results are assembled into
four-dimensional arrays of shape $(n_L,\, n_{Vgs},\, n_{Vds},\, n_{Vsb})$.
The following derived quantities are appended:

\begin{equation}
  \frac{g_m}{I_D},\quad \frac{I_D}{W},\quad \frac{g_m}{g_{ds}},\quad
  f_T = \frac{g_m}{2\pi C_{gg}}.
\end{equation}

Both NMOS and PMOS LUTs are saved to compressed NumPy archives
(\texttt{nmos\_lut.npz} and \texttt{pmos\_lut.npz}).

\subsection{Query Modes}

\texttt{query\_lut.py} supports two usage modes.

\textbf{Bias mode:} Provide $(L, V_{GS}, V_{DS})$ and get all operating-point
quantities back.  Use this to check what a transistor looks like at a
specific bias point.

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py --l 1.0 --vgs 0.8 --vds 0.9
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Design mode:} Provide a $g_m/I_D$ target, $L$, and $V_{DS}$, and
optionally a target $g_m$ or $I_D$.  The tool finds the $V_{GS}$ that hits
the efficiency target, reads the current density from the LUT, and returns
the required width using \eqref{eq:width}.

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py --l 1.0 --gmid 15 --vds 0.9 \
  --gm 628e-6
\end{lstlisting}

The design mode output includes $V_{GS}$, $I_D$, $W$, $g_m/g_{ds}$,
$V_{dsat}$, and $f_T$ --- all the values needed to verify a design decision
and move on to the next transistor.

\section{Application: Folded-Cascode Class-AB Amplifier}
\label{sec:folded_cascode}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Topology Overview}

The folded-cascode amplifier is a single-stage op-amp topology that achieves
high DC gain without the voltage headroom problem of an all-NMOS or all-PMOS
cascode~\cite{razavi2001,allen2011}.  Its core idea is to take the output
current of the differential input pair and ``fold'' it into cascode transistors
of the opposite polarity, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{folded_cascode_schematic.png}}
  \caption{Folded-cascode class-AB operational amplifier showing the
           differential input pair (M1--M3), PMOS folding current sources
           (M4, M5), cascode stacks (M6--M9), floating current mirror (FCM),
           and class-AB push-pull output stage (M10, M11).}
  \label{fig:schematic}
\end{figure}

The transistors and their roles are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{M1, M2} (NMOS differential pair): The input transistors.
        Their $g_m$ determines the GBW of the amplifier.
  \item \textbf{M3} (NMOS tail current source): Sets the total quiescent
        current through M1 and M2.
  \item \textbf{M4, M5} (PMOS folding current sources): Supply a fixed
        current into each folding node.  The difference between this current
        and M1/M2's drain current becomes the signal current in the output
        branch.
  \item \textbf{M6, M7} (NMOS cascode transistors): Stacked below the output
        node to increase the NMOS output resistance.
  \item \textbf{M8, M9} (PMOS cascode transistors): Stacked above the output
        node to increase the PMOS output resistance.
  \item \textbf{M10, M11} (NMOS/PMOS class-AB output pair): A push-pull output
        stage driven by the folded-cascode output.  M10 sources current when
        the output swings low; M11 sinks current when the output swings high.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Gain and Output Resistance}

The open-loop voltage gain of the folded-cascode first stage is

\begin{equation}
  A_v = g_{m1} \cdot R_{out},
  \label{eq:Av}
\end{equation}

where $R_{out}$ is the total resistance at the output node.  Looking down
into the NMOS cascode stack the resistance is approximately

\begin{equation}
  R_{out,n} \approx \frac{g_{m6}}{g_{ds6}} \cdot \frac{1}{g_{ds1}},
\end{equation}

and looking up into the PMOS cascode stack

\begin{equation}
  R_{out,p} \approx \frac{g_{m8}}{g_{ds8}} \cdot \frac{1}{g_{ds4}}.
\end{equation}

The total output resistance is $R_{out} = R_{out,n} \| R_{out,p}$, and the
gain follows from \eqref{eq:Av}.  Note that $g_m/g_{ds}$ is the
\textit{intrinsic gain} of a single transistor and is available directly
from the LUT under the field \texttt{gm\_gds}.  A target of
$A_v > 60\,\text{dB}$ (i.e., $> 1000\,\text{V/V}$) requires that both
cascode stacks contribute sufficient intrinsic gain.

\subsection{Gain-Bandwidth Product}

Assuming the load capacitance $C_L$ creates the dominant pole at the output
node, the unity-gain bandwidth is

\begin{equation}
  \text{GBW} = \frac{g_{m1}}{2\pi C_L}.
  \label{eq:GBW}
\end{equation}

This is the most important equation for starting the design because it
directly gives the required $g_m$ of the input pair:

\begin{equation}
  g_{m1,\text{req}} = 2\pi \cdot \text{GBW} \cdot C_L.
  \label{eq:gm1req}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Slew Rate}

The slew rate is limited by how quickly the circuit can charge or discharge
$C_L$.  At quiescent, the output branch carries current $I_{out}$.
Under a large input step, all of the tail current is steered to one side,
giving

\begin{equation}
  \text{SR} = \frac{I_{tail}}{C_L}.
  \label{eq:SR}
\end{equation}

For the class-AB output stage, the peak output current can be much larger
than the quiescent current, which is the advantage of the class-AB topology
over a simple class-A buffer.

\subsection{Current Budget}

Before querying the LUT for any transistor, it helps to establish the current
budget --- how much current flows through each branch at quiescent.  Using
the convention that $I_{D1}$ is the drain current of each input transistor:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Tail current source M3: $I_{tail} = 2 I_{D1}$
  \item Each side of the differential pair (M1 or M2): $I_{D1}$
  \item Each PMOS folder (M4 or M5): $I_{D4} = I_{D1} + I_{cas}$, where
        $I_{cas}$ is the quiescent current in the output cascode stack.
        A common choice is $I_{cas} = I_{D1}$, giving $I_{D4} = 2I_{D1}$.
  \item Each output cascode transistor (M6--M9): $I_{cas} = I_{D1}$
  \item Output stage (M10, M11): quiescent current $I_q$ set separately by
        the class-AB bias circuit.
\end{itemize}

% ---- Step 1 ----
\subsubsection{Step 1: Compute Required Input-Pair Transconductance}

Apply \eqref{eq:gm1req} to find the transconductance the input pair must
provide:

\begin{equation}
  g_{m1,\text{req}} = 2\pi \times 10\,\text{MHz} \times 10\,\text{pF}
                     \approx 628\,\mu\text{A/V}.
\end{equation}

This number is fixed by the specification and does not change regardless of
which transistor is chosen.  Everything else follows from it.

% ---- Step 2 ----
\subsubsection{Step 2: Choose gm/Id and L for the Input Pair (M1, M2)}

Look at the \textbf{$I_D/W$ vs.\ gm/Id} plot (Fig.~\ref{fig:id_wl})
and the \textbf{$f_T$ vs.\ gm/Id} plot (Fig.~\ref{fig:ft}) to pick an
operating point for M1/M2.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Choose $g_m/I_D = 15\,\text{V}^{-1}$ (moderate inversion --- good
        balance of efficiency and bandwidth).
  \item Choose $L = 1.0\,\mu\text{m}$ (longer channel for better intrinsic
        gain; check that the \texttt{gm\_gds} value is large enough after
        the query).
  \item Assume $V_{DS1} \approx 0.9\,\text{V}$ for the query.  This is a
        reasonable mid-supply estimate; you will verify it fits once the
        full bias point is known.
\end{itemize}

Run the design-mode query, passing the required $g_m$:

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --l 1.0 --gmid 15 --vds 0.9 \
  --gm 628e-6
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{What to record from the output:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $V_{GS1}$ --- you will need this to set the bias network.
  \item $I_{D1}$ --- the quiescent drain current of each input transistor.
  \item $W_{1,2}$ --- the transistor width the tool computed from
        \eqref{eq:width}.
  \item $V_{dsat1}$ --- needed for the output swing headroom check in
        Step~8.
\end{itemize}

% ---- Step 3 ----
\subsubsection{Step 3: Size the Tail Current Source (M3)}

The tail source must supply $I_{tail} = 2 I_{D1}$.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use NMOS.  Choose a long channel ($L = 2.0\,\mu\text{m}$) to
        maximize $r_o$, which increases the common-mode rejection ratio.
  \item The tail source is not in the signal path, so its $g_m/I_D$ is
        chosen to keep $V_{dsat}$ small (preserving headroom at the bottom
        of the supply) while ensuring saturation.  A value of
        $g_m/I_D \approx 10\,\text{V}^{-1}$ is a reasonable starting point.
  \item Assume $V_{DS,tail} \approx 0.3\,\text{V}$ --- just enough to keep
        it in saturation.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --l 2.0 --gmid 10 --vds 0.3 \
  --id <2 * ID1 from Step 2>
\end{lstlisting}

Record $V_{GS3}$, $W_3$, and $V_{dsat3}$.

% ---- Step 4 ----
\subsubsection{Step 4: Size the PMOS Folding Current Sources (M4, M5)}

Each PMOS folder supplies $I_{D4} = 2 I_{D1}$ (one $I_{D1}$ for the
differential pair branch and one $I_{D1}$ for the output cascode branch).

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use the PMOS LUT (\texttt{--path char\_data/pmos\_lut.npz}).
  \item Choose $L = 1.0\,\mu\text{m}$.
  \item Choose $g_m/I_D \approx 12\,\text{V}^{-1}$ (moderate inversion).
        The PMOS folders are current sources, not signal transistors, but
        their $r_o$ appears in $R_{out,p}$ so a longer channel or moderate
        inversion is preferred.
  \item Assume $V_{SD4} \approx 0.9\,\text{V}$ (note: for PMOS the tool
        uses $V_{SG}$ and $V_{SD}$ internally, but the command-line
        arguments \texttt{--vgs} and \texttt{--vds} refer to the intrinsic
        quantities, so enter positive magnitudes the same way as for NMOS).
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --path char_data/pmos_lut.npz \
  --l 1.0 --gmid 12 --vds 0.9 \
  --id <2 * ID1>
\end{lstlisting}

Record $V_{SG4}$, $W_{4,5}$, and $V_{dsat4}$.

% ---- Step 5 ----
\subsubsection{Step 5: Size the NMOS Cascode Transistors (M6, M7)}

M6/M7 carry the output cascode current $I_{cas} = I_{D1}$ and are in the
signal path.  Their intrinsic gain $g_{m6}/g_{ds6}$ (field \texttt{gm\_gds}
in the LUT output) multiplies $r_{o1}$ to form $R_{out,n}$.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use NMOS.  Choose $L = 1.0\,\mu\text{m}$ or longer if
        \texttt{gm\_gds} is too low.
  \item Choose $g_m/I_D \approx 15\,\text{V}^{-1}$.
  \item For $V_{DS6}$: the cascode transistor is biased so that M1 is
        just at the edge of saturation, meaning $V_{DS6} \approx V_{dsat6}$.
        This maximizes output swing.  Use $V_{DS6} = 0.2\,\text{V}$ as an
        initial approximation and refine after checking $V_{dsat6}$ from the
        query output.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --l 1.0 --gmid 15 --vds 0.2 \
  --id <ID1>
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Check:} Inspect the \texttt{gm\_gds} value.  If
$R_{out,n} = (\texttt{gm\_gds})_6 \times r_{o1}$ combined with $R_{out,p}$
does not give enough gain (Step~8), increase $L$ and re-query.

Record $V_{GS6}$, $W_{6,7}$, $V_{dsat6}$, and \texttt{gm\_gds}.

% ---- Step 6 ----
\subsubsection{Step 6: Size the PMOS Cascode Transistors (M8, M9)}

M8/M9 are the mirror image of M6/M7 on the PMOS side and carry the same
current $I_{cas} = I_{D1}$.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Use the PMOS LUT.
  \item Match the $g_m/I_D$ and $L$ choices made for M6/M7.
  \item Use $V_{SD8} = 0.2\,\text{V}$ (same reasoning as Step~5).
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --path char_data/pmos_lut.npz \
  --l 1.0 --gmid 15 --vds 0.2 \
  --id <ID1>
\end{lstlisting}

Record $V_{SG8}$, $W_{8,9}$, $V_{dsat8}$, and \texttt{gm\_gds}.

% ---- Step 7 ----
\subsubsection{Step 7: Estimate DC Gain}

With all cascode transistors sized, estimate $A_v$ to verify the
$60\,\text{dB}$ target before proceeding.

The output conductance of M1 is $g_{ds1} = g_{m1} / \texttt{gm\_gds}_1$
(read \texttt{gm\_gds} from the Step~2 query).  Then:

\begin{align}
  R_{out,n} &= (\texttt{gm\_gds})_6 \times (1/g_{ds1}), \\
  R_{out,p} &= (\texttt{gm\_gds})_8 \times (1/g_{ds4}), \\
  R_{out}   &= R_{out,n} \| R_{out,p}, \\
  A_v       &= g_{m1} \times R_{out}.
\end{align}

If $A_v < 1000\,\text{V/V}$, increase the channel length of the cascode
transistors (M6--M9) to raise \texttt{gm\_gds} and re-query.  Longer
channels increase intrinsic gain but reduce $f_T$; the design space for
$L$ is shown in the LUT plots.

% ---- Step 8 ----
\subsubsection{Step 8: Check Output Swing Headroom}

Each cascode stack removes a minimum of two $V_{dsat}$ values from the
available output swing.  The minimum output voltage is

\begin{equation}
  V_{out,\min} = V_{dsat6} + V_{dsat1},
  \label{eq:vout_min}
\end{equation}

and the maximum output voltage is

\begin{equation}
  V_{out,\max} = V_{DD} - V_{dsat8} - V_{dsat4}.
  \label{eq:vout_max}
\end{equation}

Verify that $V_{out,\max} - V_{out,\min}$ meets the $\pm 1\,\text{V}$
swing target around mid-rail ($1.65\,\text{V}$ for a $3.3\,\text{V}$
supply).  If not, reduce the $g_m/I_D$ of the cascode and folding devices
toward strong inversion to shrink their $V_{dsat}$ values and re-run
Steps~4--6.

% ---- Step 9 ----
\subsubsection{Step 9: Size the Class-AB Output Stage (M10, M11)}

The class-AB output stage is a push-pull pair (M10 NMOS, M11 PMOS) driven
directly by the folded-cascode output.  The class-AB bias circuit sets a
quiescent current $I_q$ through both devices while allowing them to source
or sink much larger currents under a large signal.

\paragraph{Determine $I_q$:}
The quiescent transconductance of the output stage determines the
high-frequency output impedance seen by $C_L$.  A common starting point
is to set $g_{m,out} \approx 3$--$5 \times g_{m1}$ so that the output
stage pole ($f \approx g_{m,out} / (2\pi C_L)$) is well above the
unity-gain frequency.  Choose $I_q$ to achieve this.

\paragraph{Size M10 (NMOS):}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Use NMOS LUT.  Choose $g_m/I_D \approx 10\,\text{V}^{-1}$ (strong
        inversion for low $V_{dsat}$ to maximize output swing).
  \item Choose $L = 0.7\,\mu\text{m}$ (shorter for lower $V_{dsat}$, but
        not minimum length to avoid poor $r_o$).
  \item Assume $V_{DS10} \approx V_{DD}/2$ at quiescent.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --l 0.7 --gmid 10 --vds 1.65 \
  --id <Iq>
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Size M11 (PMOS):}
Run the same query against the PMOS LUT with the same $g_m/I_D$ target and
$I_q$:

\begin{lstlisting}
python query_lut.py \
  --path char_data/pmos_lut.npz \
  --l 0.7 --gmid 10 --vds 1.65 \
  --id <Iq>
\end{lstlisting}

Record $V_{GS10}$, $V_{SG11}$, $W_{10}$, $W_{11}$, and both $V_{dsat}$ values.

% ---- Step 10 ----
\subsubsection{Step 10: Determine Bias Voltages}

Each transistor's $V_{GS}$ (or $V_{SG}$ for PMOS) returned by the LUT
tells you the gate voltage required for that device to carry its target
current.  Use these to set the fixed bias voltages in the schematic:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{M3 gate} ($V_{b3}$): set so $V_{b3} = V_{GS3}$.
  \item \textbf{M6/M7 NMOS cascode gate} ($V_{b6}$): the cascode gate must be
        high enough that M6/M7 are saturated while M1/M2 are also
        saturated.  A starting value is
        $V_{b6} = V_{dsat1} + V_{GS6}$ (bias M6 so that when M1's drain
        sits at $V_{dsat1}$, M6's $V_{GS} = V_{GS6}$).
  \item \textbf{M8/M9 PMOS cascode gate} ($V_{b8}$): by symmetry,
        $V_{b8} = V_{DD} - V_{dsat4} - V_{SG8}$.
  \item \textbf{M4/M5 PMOS folder gate} ($V_{b4}$): set so
        $V_{DD} - V_{b4} = V_{SG4}$.
\end{itemize}

In practice these voltages are generated by a bias network (e.g., a diode-
connected transistor or a simple resistor divider).  At this stage,
enter the calculated numerical values directly as voltage sources in LTspice
to verify the operating point before adding a proper bias network.

% ---- Step 11 ----
\subsubsection{Step 11: Verify in LTspice}

Place all transistors in the schematic using the widths and lengths from
Steps~2--9.  Apply the bias voltages from Step~10.  Run a \texttt{.op}
simulation and check:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Each transistor is in saturation: $V_{DS} > V_{dsat}$ (NMOS) or
        $V_{SD} > V_{dsat}$ (PMOS).
  \item The drain currents match the current budget from
        Section~\ref{sec:folded_cascode}C.
  \item The output node DC voltage sits near mid-supply when both inputs
        are at the same voltage (common mode).
\end{enumerate}

If a transistor is in the linear region, adjust its bias voltage or widen
the device to reduce $V_{dsat}$ and re-run.  Once the \texttt{.op} point
looks correct, run an \texttt{.ac} analysis and verify GBW against
\eqref{eq:GBW}.

Four plots generated by \texttt{tiwari\_plots.py} illustrate the NMOS
design space.  All curves are parametrized by channel length
$L \in \{0.35, 0.70, 1.00, 2.00\}\,\mu\text{m}$.

\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:id_wl} --- $I_D/W$ vs.\ gm/Id:} This is the
primary sizing chart used in \eqref{eq:width}.  Given a $g_m/I_D$ target,
read the current density $I_D/W$ from the vertical axis.  Dividing the
required drain current by this density gives the transistor width directly.
Shorter channels carry more current per micron at the same $g_m/I_D$ target,
which means a narrower device but at the cost of lower intrinsic gain.

\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:gmgds} --- $g_m/g_{ds}$ vs.\ gm/Id:} This plot shows
the intrinsic gain of a single transistor as a function of inversion level.
Use it during Step~7 to estimate the cascode output resistance.  Longer
channels provide substantially higher intrinsic gain; the $L = 2.0\,\mu\text{m}$
curve peaks near $650\,\text{V/V}$ at moderate inversion.

\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:gmgds_vds} --- $g_m r_o$ vs.\ $V_{DS}$:} Intrinsic
gain also depends on $V_{DS}$.  This plot (at fixed $V_{ov} = 200\,\text{mV}$)
shows that gain peaks around $V_{DS} \approx 2\,\text{V}$ and falls at low
$V_{DS}$ (where the device is near the triode boundary) and at high $V_{DS}$
(where channel-length modulation increases $g_{ds}$).  Use it to verify that
the chosen $V_{DS}$ for each cascode transistor is in the high-gain region.

\textbf{Fig.~\ref{fig:ft} --- $f_T$ vs.\ gm/Id:} Use this to verify that
the transistors are fast enough for the target GBW.  As a rule of thumb,
aim for $f_T > 10 \times \text{GBW}$ for each transistor in the signal
path to keep parasitic poles well above unity gain.  $f_T$ peaks in moderate
inversion and drops rapidly toward weak inversion.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A Python-based gm/Id characterization and query tool has been presented.
The tool automates the characterization sweep, stores results in a
four-dimensional LUT, and provides a design-mode query that converts a
$g_m/I_D$ target and required current into a transistor width in a single
step.  The methodology was demonstrated through an eleven-step sizing
procedure for a folded-cascode class-AB operational amplifier.  Because all
sizing data come directly from the SPICE model, the results are more accurate
than square-law estimates, and no hand-calculation iteration is required
before running the verification simulation.
